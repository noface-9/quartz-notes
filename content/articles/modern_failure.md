# Why am I writing this?
For a long time, I felt that I had failed to become any sort of genius. My fascination with business often turned into endless overthinking rather than meaningful action. I never considered myself exceptionally smart in any particular domain. Watching people perform complex mental calculations or grasp concepts effortlessly made me wonder where I had gone wrong. I found myself inventing explanations for why I had reached the limits of my own abilities.

Then I came across a fascinating article.

It challenged the narrow definition of intelligence that I had been holding onto and offered me a different perspective. For the first time in a while, it gave me hope — not that I needed to become someone else's version of a genius, but that I could discover and cultivate my own.

# From Symbols to Structure: My Biggest Learning

### 1. Looking at "Cognitive Limit"

When I say I reached a cognitive limit, it's just a representational limit. When I use one kind of thinking and apply it to wrong set of problems, it's inevitable for me to think I'm stupid. 

Feeling "stupid" doesn't always mean you've reached your cognitive limit.

Sometimes you've simply reached the point where the **kind of thinking required has changed.**

---

### 2. Hierarchy of Challenges

#### Cognitive Limits vs Intelligence

**Don't confuse intelligence with training.**

A degree doesn't prove someone is smarter—it shows **what kinds of mental skills they have spent years developing.**

Think of the brain like a muscle:

* Engineering trains mathematical, logical, and abstract reasoning.
* Literature trains interpretation, writing, and critical analysis.
* Different disciplines train different cognitive abilities.

#### The Difficulty Spectrum

As the difficulty of a problem increases:

Easy problem → Almost everyone can solve it.
Medium problem → Fewer people can solve it well.
Extremely hard problem → Only a tiny number of experts can even understand or work on it.

The hardest problems (e.g., the Riemann Hypothesis) sit at the edge of human cognitive capability.

#### Insight

**The ability to solve a problem depends on both:**

1. **Raw cognitive ability** (reasoning capacity), and
2. **Years of specialized training and knowledge.**

As problems become harder, fewer people possess both.

**Remember:** Harder problems reveal the limits of human cognition—not the worth of a person. 

---

### 3. There are two ways of thinking.

#### **Symbolic Thinking**

* Learn rules, formulas, facts, procedures.
* Solve known problems.
* Rewards speed, accuracy and memorization.
* "Apply the method."

#### **Structural Thinking**

* Understand relationships, constraints and underlying principles.
* Discover or redefine the problem itself.
* Rewards curiosity, patience and intuition.
* "Why does this work?" and "Is there another way?"

*Education mostly trains symbolic thinking.*

Schools reward getting the right answer quickly.

The world's hardest problems require **structural thinking**—seeing patterns, questioning assumptions and creating new ways of thinking.

---

### 4. Expertise & Creativity

Being good at solving textbook problems is different from creating new knowledge.

At higher levels, success depends less on remembering techniques and more on developing intuition.

**Creative people often become great mathematicians.**

Creativity isn't separate from mathematics — it helps people see structures others miss. Many exceptional mathematicians were also artists, musicians or poets 

(As odd as it sounds, mathematics is a creative pursuit with profound imagination & out-of-the-box thinking)

We need to develop slow thinking. Deep ideas are rarely produced by quick thinking. Real breakthroughs often come after long periods of confusion, exploration and reflection.

---

### 5. Developing Structural Thinking 

Easy problems ask:

> "How do I solve this?"

Hard problems ask:

> "What is the real problem?"
> "Does a solution even exist?"
> "Am I looking at this the right way?"

---

## Lessons for Life

* Don't mistake confusion for lack of intelligence.
* Learn principles, not just procedures.
* Seek understanding before memorization.
* Read widely — art, philosophy, science and history build intuition.
* Be comfortable being slow.
* The goal isn't to know more facts; it's to see deeper structures.

### One sentence to remember forever:

> **Knowledge teaches you the rules. Structural thinking teaches you when the rules no longer apply.**
