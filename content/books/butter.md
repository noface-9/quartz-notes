# Why am I writing this?
I received this novel as a gift from my dearest friends, which made the reading experience even more special. I devoured it in just a few sittings—I simply couldn't put it down and found myself wanting to return to it whenever I had to step away.

One delightful surprise was the many references to **Julia Child** throughout the French cooking scenes. As someone who enjoys cooking, I thoroughly appreciated those moments; they added an extra layer of richness to an already immersive story.

---

## Themes & Key Takeaways

### 1. Looking Beyond First Impressions

One of the first things I noticed was how the novel introduces every character from an outsider's perspective. They are initially defined by visible traits, social roles, and public opinion. Kajii is first seen as an "ugly, overweight, middle-aged woman" who somehow manipulated wealthy men, while Reiko is simply a housewife who enjoys cooking, and Rika is an ambitious journalist.

As the story unfolds, these labels slowly fall apart. We begin to understand the childhoods, relationships, insecurities, and experiences that shaped each person.

**Lot of people seem boring and dull on the outside, but there are so many beautiful pieces within them (also, there is no one boring - just undiscovered). I want to remember that people are far more complicated than the stories society tells about them.**

---

### 2. We Are Products of Our Upbringing

Every major character is deeply influenced by the environment they grew up in.

- Reiko's love for cooking stems from being raised by a maid (and not her rich parents)
- Rika's emotionally distant, "prince-like" personality reflects her upbringing and expectations placed upon her (the adoration she recieves from her peers)
- Kajii's confidence, entitlement, and manipulative charm are inseparable from being adored and treated like a princess by her father (later on by people she seduces, it's a self-fulfilling prophecy)

**The novel doesn't ask us to excuse people's actions, but to recognize that every person carries a history that shapes who they become. It's easy to pinpoint things we like or dislike about a person, whereas understanding where they are coming from is a Herculean task (not everyone can do it).**

---

### 3. Misogyny, Fatphobia & Society's Double Standards

Novel's strongest critiques is directed toward society's expectations of women.

Kajii refuses to conform to ideals of being thin, restrained, modest, and self-denying. She unapologetically enjoys rich food, desires pleasure, and believes herself worthy of admiration.

Ironically, society appears more disturbed by her body and confidence than by the murders she allegedly committed. Kajii's body becomes a mirror reflecting society's prejudices more than her own character.

The novel also explores how misogyny isn't perpetuated only by men. Women themselves often internalize these expectations, judging one another's weight, appearance, and life choices to conform to social norms.

**This made me reflect on my own life and the many ways societal expectations quietly shape our choices. I can recognize moments where I've felt the pressure of prejudice, double standards, or the need to fit into predefined moulds. One lesson I'm taking away is to become more conscious of these influences — to pause, question whether they truly align with my own values, and make decisions that are informed by self-awareness rather than expectation.**

---

### 4. Food as Power, Identity & Self-Care

Food is never just food in *Butter*.

It represents: pleasure (Rika imaging Kajii having an orgasm with butter), comfort (cooking rice, butter & soy sauce), authenticity (getting mad over "beef stew"), desire (having noodles post sex), nourishment (wanting to fill the body with butter), rebellion (questioning why do women make themselves smaller), identity (against societal expetations)

Kajii teaches Rika to slow down and actually enjoy eating instead of surviving on convenience store meals. Cooking becomes an act of caring for oneself rather than merely fulfilling a necessity.

Rich food becomes an act of resistance against a society that expects women to constantly restrict themselves.

**Everyone can have their own definition of richness and luxury with regards to food. I look at it as a force of vitality instead of rebellion.**

---

### 5. Butter as a Central Symbol

Butter weaves through the novel as both a literal ingredient and a symbolic thread.

It represents:
- indulgence over restraint
- authenticity over imitation (butter versus margarine)
- nourishment instead of deprivation
- pleasure without guilt
- luxury and abundance
- finding one's own "right amount"

The symbolism stretches even further through references to blood becoming butter, seasonal richness, dairy products tasting better in winter, and even discussions about cows existing independently of bulls.

**I see butter as a metaphor for anything we choose to embrace out of genuine desire rather than social expectation. It represents the freedom to cultivate our own tastes instead of inheriting someone else's.**

---

### 6. Truth Depends on Perspective

No single narrator tells the whole story.

Kajii constantly shapes her own narrative, portraying herself as misunderstood and uniquely worthy of admiration. Meanwhile, mothers, sisters, neighbours, classmates, stalkers, and victims' families reveal entirely different versions of the same events.

Reiko's careful observations frequently uncover details that Rika misses while becoming emotionally entangled with Kajii.

**Truth rarely belongs to a single person; it often emerges somewhere between multiple perspectives. We rarely see things exactly as they are. Instead, we interpret them through the lens of our own experiences, beliefs, conventions, biases, and assumptions.**

---

### 7. Obsession, Charisma & Self-Perception

Kajii lives within a reality largely constructed by her own self-image.

She genuinely believes others are captivated by her and views herself as superior to those around her. Whether these beliefs are confidence, delusion, or self-preservation remains intentionally ambiguous.

At the same time, Rika becomes increasingly fascinated by Kajii, drawn toward her despite recognizing her manipulation.

**One of the most important lessons I took from this novel is how charisma can make us overlook contradictions, blurring the line between admiration and obsession. When we deeply admire someone, we often become more willing to excuse their flaws, overlook warning signs, or accept the version of reality they present. If that admiration is exploited — if the relationship becomes a manipulative game — that realisation can break us.**

**The novel also made me wonder whether our fascination with charismatic people says as much about us as it does about them. The qualities we admire, the stories we choose to believe, and the people we are drawn to often reveal our own desires, insecurities, and unmet needs. In that sense, understanding why someone captivates us can be just as important as understanding the person themselves.**

---

### 8. Female Friendship & Emotional Intimacy

While Kajii's relationships are built on manipulation, Reiko and Rika offer a quieter, healthier form of companionship.

Their friendship is supportive, patient, and dependable, yet not without flaws. Both women keep parts of themselves hidden from one another and from those closest to them.

The novel suggests that love and loyalty do not automatically lead to complete emotional openness.

**One thought that stayed with me was that Kajii, too, might have found growth and healing had she chosen genuine friendship over manipulation. Instead, she kept everyone at a distance, treating relationships as games to be won. In contrast, Rika's transformation is made possible because she was open to experiences with both Kajii & Reiko. Their friendship gives her the space to grow, heal, and rediscover herself. The novel reminds us that the people we surround ourselves with profoundly shape who we become.**

---

### 9. The Invisible Prisons Women Live In

The novel repeatedly returns to the many roles women are expected to perform.

They are expected to be:
- desirable but not indulgent,
- independent but nurturing,
- ambitious but family-oriented,
- attractive but never excessive.

Whether through marriage, motherhood, careers, or beauty standards, every woman in the story struggles with some form of invisible confinement.

**In the end, we can either continue blaming society for the roles it assigns us, or consciously choose the roles we want to play. We cannot control the expectations but we can definitely decide which ones to accept.**

---

### 10. The Dark Side of True Crime

The novel quietly critiques not only our fascination with sensationalized crime, but also the societal structures that make stories like Kajii's possible.

The media and public become obsessed with Kajii's recipes, body, appearance, and eating habits, reducing her to a spectacle rather than trying to understand the social and psychological forces surrounding the case. She becomes a caricature instead of a person.

Alongside this, the novel repeatedly depicts older men searching for women to care for them—to cook, nurture, and provide companionship. It subtly questions why these responsibilities continue to be assumed as women's work, and why so many men struggle to care for themselves independently.

Rather than presenting a conventional murder mystery, *Butter* asks a deeper question: **Why are we so captivated by women like Kajii, and what does that fascination reveal about us?** It challenges us to examine not only the woman at the center of the story, but also the society that created, judged, and consumed her.


---

### 11. Learning Your Own Taste

By the end, the novel feels less like a crime story and more like a meditation on self-understanding.

The most meaningful takeaway wasn't about Kajii—it was about ourselves.

Life, much like cooking, cannot be measured by someone else's recipe.

We need to discover:
- what nourishes us,
- what brings us joy,
- what is too much,
- what is enough,
- and how to care for ourselves without constantly seeking external validation.

We can choose what we want to do with our life. But also that life has plans & tastes of its own. 

**Telugu reference to a festive dish that blends six distinct ingredients to represent the emotional spectrum of life.**

- Jaggery (sweetness) - Happiness
- Raw Mango (Tangy) - Surprise
- Tamarind (Sourness) - Challenges
- Neem Flower (Bitterness) - Sadness
- Chilli (Spiciness) - Anger
- Salt (Salty) - Fear

The idea of finding the "right amount" becomes the novel's quiet philosophy—not just in food, but in relationships, ambition, identity, and life itself. 

We cannot just take the happy parts and discard everything else. All different ingredients make the dish yummy. If Reiko never went through sad parts, she'd never be able to disocver herself. Let life pass through. 


> **Final Reflection:**  
> *Butter* is a layered exploration of identity, appetite, womanhood, perception, and freedom. Beneath its true crime premise lies a thoughtful examination of how society judges women, how stories shape reality, and how genuine liberation begins when we learn to trust our own tastes over everyone else's.
