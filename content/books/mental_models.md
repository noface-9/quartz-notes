## Vol 1: General Thinking Concepts

> The key to better understanding the world is to build a latticework of mental models. 

*You only think you know, as a matter of fact. And most of your actions are based on incomplete knowledge and you really don't know what it is all about, or what the purpose of the world is, or know a great deal of other things. It is possible to live and not know.*

### Acquiring wisdom

> I believe in the discipline of mastering the best of what other people have figured out. 

- All of us have **subconscious mental models** which affect the way we think and reason, infer causality, match patterns, and draw analogies. 
- To see a problem for what it is, we must first **break it down to substantive parts** so the interconnections are clear. 
- **When understanding is separated from reality - we lose powers** (just like Antaeus lost to Heracles after losing contact with his mother, Earth)
- Everything I learn **must be tested** against reality & updated accordingly. Continuous process.
- It's hard to understand a system that we are part of because we have **blind spots**.

##### How do we fail to update even after interacting with reality? 
1. Not having the right perspective or vantage point - hard time seeing any system that we are in (dropping a ball in a moving ship - vertical and horizontal change in position). 
2. Ego-induced denial - invested in our opinions of ourselves to see the world's feedback (to update our beliefs about reality)
    * Afraid about what others will say about us - we don't put our ideas out there and subject them to criticism. 
    * If we do put our ideas out there and they are criticized - our ego steps in to protect us. 
    * We become invested in defending instead of upgrading our ideas.
3. Distance from consequences of our decisions - easier to keep our current views rather than update them. 
    * Further we are from the results/feedback of our decisions, easier to convince ourselves that we are right and avoid the challenge, the pain of updating our views. 

> A man who has committed a mistake and doesn't correct it, is committing another mistake. 

> Most geniuses - especially those who lead others - prosper not by deconstructing intricate complexities but by exploiting unrecognized simplicities. 

We not only understand the immediate problem with more accuracy, but we can begin to see the second-, third-, and higher-order consequences. 

##### Three buckets of Knowledge: 
1. Inorganic Systems - Laws of math and physics, the entire physical universe
2. Organic Systems - Biology on Earth
3. Human History 

##### Explaining Blind Spots
Think of a forest
- Botanist - Ecosystem
- Environmentalist - Impact of Climate change
- Forestry Engineer - State of tree growth
- Business persoon - Value of the land

> Well, the first rule is that you can't really know anything if you just remember isolated facts and try and bang'em back. If the facts don't hang together on a latticework of theory, you don't have them in usable form. You've got to have models in your head. Have to array your experience both vicarious and direct on this latticework on models.

Using and failing, as long as I acknowledge, reflect, and learn from it, is also how I can build my repertoire.

### 1. The Map is not the territory

**The description of the thing is not the thing itself**

##### Features of a Map
1. Maps have a structure similar or dissimilar to the territory - they desribe in a *useful* way with *specific purpose*. Not everything to everyone. 
2. Similar structures have similary "logical" characteristics. 
3. Map is not the actual territory. Can't experience how it's like in the territory as it is. Nor can be used for another purpose.
4. Ideal map - will have another map to explain it, which will have another map. Then you'd never have any issues but eventually it's overwhelming. 

###### Extra Notes 
News is an abstraction created by other people. Models and abstractions fail to understand their limits. 
Problem ‼️ - If our knowledge becomes of the map, rather than actual underlying territory it describes. 
We mistake map for reality and think we have all the answers, create static rules or policies that deal with the map - but forget we exist in a changing world. 
**We have to udnerstand the limitations of maps & models**

##### Important considerations to use a map accurately:
1. Reality is the ultimate update - feedback loops by the explorers
2. Consider the Cartographer - Not objective - reflects the values, standards & limitations of their creators (Western influence in modern maps)
3. Maps can influence territories - Elaborate models of design without paying attention to how things work in reality

### 2. Circle of Competence

> I'm no genius. I'm smart in spots - but I stay around those spots

When ego and not competence drives what we undertake, we have blind spots. 

##### Questions answered:
1. What is a circle of competence? Lifer vs Stranger concept. 
2. How do you know when you have one? We know exactly what we don't know. Can make quick and relatively accurate decisions. Know what is knowable, unknowable & can distinguish between both. Can anticipate and respond to objections because we've heard them before. Already put in all the work to counter them. 
3. How do you build and maintain one? Not overnight or few months. Years of experience, of making mistakes, and of actively seeking out better methods of practice and thought. Never take it for granted - it's not static - that *once attained is attained for life* ❌ World is dynamic. Knowledge gets updated - so must the circle. 
4. How do you operate outside of one? Learn basics. Acknowledge that we are Stranger not Lifer; List of detailed thoughtful questions to make a good decision. Broad understanding - find useful foundational concepts. 


###### Extra Notes on above Q&A: 
> We shall be unable to turn natural advantage to account unless we make use of local guides

> A little learning is a dangerous thing; drink deep, or taste not the Pierian spring; 
> There shallow draughts intoxicate the brains, and drinking largely sobers us again.

**Key Practices to build & maintain a Circle of Competence**
- Curiosity
- Desire to learn
- Learning - when experience meets reflection
- Monitoring - track records; courage to monitor honestly; Painful for ego which is also why it helps build a Circle of Competence. 
- Feedback - used to our advantage; Need to understand outside views; Understand where our skills are sub-optimal; Ability to give objective
 feedback to others; 

> Learn from the mistakes of others. You can't live long enough to make them all yourself. 

**Difficulty with overconfidence** - We are much worse drivers, lovers, managers, traders (many other things) that we think we are. 
We have a difficulty with honest self-reporting. We don't want to know what we're good at and bad at. Ego is a powerful enemy when it comes to better understanding reality. 

Need to be honest about failures in order to reflect and learn from them. 
*Journal self-feedback: What went wrong? How could I do better?* - Allows to see patterns that we couldn't see before. 

We have too many biases - not possible to build Circle of Competence without outside perspective.

### Additional information 
**Problem of Incentives** - Skews how much we can rely on someone else's circle of competence. They have their own incentives for all the advice given out. 

> Ignorance more often begets confidence than knowledge

**Theory of Falsifiability by Karl Popper**
A theory is part of empirical science if and only if it conflicts with possible experiences and is therefore in principle falsifiable by experience.
A scientific theory must be testable in a way that allows reality to prove it wrong. If nothing could ever disprove it, it's not science.
Good theory must have an element of risk (of being wrong) to it. 

**Questioning different scenarios**

Example of *Historicism*: Belief that history follows fixed laws, and if we discover those laws, we can predict the future.
Statement: There is a Law of Increasing Technological Complexity. 
Question: What observation would prove this law false?
Someone could reply:
"That's just a temporary setback."
Suppose it stays simple for 50 years.
They could say:
"The long-term trend is still increasing complexity."
No matter what happens, the theory survives.
That means it isn't really testable.
 
*Calling them "laws"?*
These "laws" are interpretations of history - not scientifically established laws. 

*Immune to flasifying evidence*
If a theory predicts all outcomes, no point of the theory. 

*Any new evidence is interpreted through the lens of the theory.*
Science should work like this:
Theory → Evidence → Change theory if necessary.
Not:
Theory → Twist evidence → Keep theory forever.

**If a theory can explain every possible outcome, it explains nothing. A scientific theory must allow reality the chance to prove it wrong.**

### 3. First Principles Thinking

> Richard Feyman: Idk what's the matter with people; they don't learn by understanding - they learn by rote. Their knowledge is fragile

Best ways to reverse-engineer complicated situations and unleash creative possibility. Separate underlying ideas or facts from any assumptions based on them. 
What remains are essentials. 

Knowledge can be built only when we are actively trying to falsify it. 

##### Techniques for Establishing First Principles
If we never learn to take something apart, test our assumptions about it, and reconstruct it - we end up bound by what other people tell us (trapped in the way the things have always been done). When environment changes - we will continue the same way - making costly mistakes along the way. 

> Everything that is not a law of nature is a shared belief. Money, national border, bitcoin, love, etc.

###### Two key techniques: Socratic Questioning & Five Whys?

**Socratic Thinking** - No gut thinking and limits emotional responses. 
1. Clarifying my thinking and explaining the origins of my ideas? (Why do I think this? What exactly do I think?)
2. Challenging assumptions. (How do I know this is true? What if I thought the opposite?)
3. Looking for evidence (How can I back this up? What are the sources?)
4. Considering alternative perspectives. (What others might think? How do I know I'm correct?)
5. Examining consequences and implications. (What if I'm wrong? What are the consequences if I am?)
6. Questioning the original questions. (Why did I think that? Was I correct? What conclusions can I draw from the remaining process?)

**First Principles** - Method rooted in the behaviour of children. 
Series of Why's - to land at "What" or "How" - Why's result in a statement of falsifiable fact - first principle
If it ends at "because I said so" or "it just is" - landed on assumption of popular opinion, myth or dogma. 

**Incremental Innovation or Paradigm Shifts** - To improve something we need to understand why it is successful or not. Even incremental improvement is hard to achieve if we can't find the first principles. 

> As to methods, there may be a million and then some, but principles are few. The man who grasps principles can successfully select his own methods. The man who tries methods, ignoring principles, is sure to have trouble.

### 4. Thought Experiment

> Creativity is intelligence having fun

Thought Experiment: Devices of imagination used to investigate the nature of things (To know what can be known)
Then come up with avenues for inquiry and exploration. Explore situations from more angles than we can physically examine and test for. 

##### Steps for a successful Thought Experimentation
1. Ask a question
2. Conduct a background research
3. Construct Hypothesis
4. Test with (thought) experiments
5. Analyze outcomes and draw conclusions
6. Compare to hypothesis and adjust accordingly (new question etc)

**Usefulness of Thought Experiements**
1. Imagining physical impossibilities
2. Re-imagining history - can't talk about the alternative effects of history, but can explore unrealized outcomes
3. Intuiting the non-intuitive - helps to verify if our natural intuition is correct by running experiments to make a point clear. 

"If money were no object" or "If you had all the time in the world"

When we think of unrealised alternatives - if we can attach an approximate probability - relative to the scope of possible ones - we can learn what the most likely outcomes are. 

**Role of Chance:** Running an experiemnt in all the possible situtations gives some real decision making power. Limit of what I know & limits of what I should attempt. How smart or stupid your decisions were regardless of the actual outcome. 

**Veil of Ignorance by John Rawls** - In Theory of Justice, to figure out the most fair and equitable way to structure society, the designers of said society operate behind a veil of ignorance. This means they could not know who they would be in the society they were creating. They have to design the society without knowing their economic status, their ethical backgrounds, talents & interests, or even their gender. Have to put in place a structure that was as fair as possible to guarantee a best outcome for themselves. 

**Necessity & Sufficiency** - Gap between the difference of becoming a published author and becoming JK Rowling
Mathematical Sets - Set {Necessary Conditions} < Set (Sufficient Conditions)

### 5. Second-Order thinking

> Technology is fine, but the scientists and engineers only partially think through their problems. they solve certain aspects, but not the total, and as a consequence it is slapping us back in the face very hard. 

Requires not only considering immediate consequences bu tht esubsequent effects of those actions as well.

*Law of Unintended Consequences*
Examples:  
1. British - Indian colonial rule of catching venomous cobras. 
2. Adding tracting to the tires - (safer, but engine works hard - worse gas mileage, detrimental carbon dioxide, rubber particles on the road)
3. Feeding antibiotics to livestock for decades
4. Standing on tip toes in the crowd

Areas where second-order thinking can be beneficial:
1. Prioritising long term interests over immediate gains (like avoiding sugar)
2. Constructing effective arguments

Point 1:
Example: Cleopatra of Egypt & Roman general Caesar - Auletes (her father) - "What Auletes passed down to her daughter was a precarious balancing act. To please one constituency was to displease other. Failure to comply with Rome would lead to intervention. Failure to stand up to Rome would lead to riots."

**Developing trust for Future Success** -
Trust & trustworthiness are the results of multiple interactions. Second-order thinking is so useful and valuable: Immediate payoff in our interactions with people, unless they are a win-win, almost always guarantees that interaction will be a one-off. 

Maximising benefits happen over time. Considering effects of the effects of our actions on others, or on our reputations, is critical to getting people to trust us, and to enjoy the benefits of cooperation that come with that. 

Point 2:
Arguments are more effective when there is higher-order of thinking involved. 

Example: Philosopher Mary Wollstonecraft - Empowering women was a first-order effect of recognizing that women had rights. 
But by discussing the logical consequences this would have on society, the second-order effects, she started a conversation that eventually resulted in what we now call feminism. Not only would women get freedoms they deserved, they would become better women, and better members of society.

⚠️ Valuable but should tempered - paralysis of Slippery Slope Effect - Action A - Slipper slope down to hell - consequences B, C, D...
If we worried about all possible effects of effects of our actions, we would likely never do anything, and we’d be wrong. How you’ll balance the need for higher-order thinking with practical, limiting judgment must be taken on a case-by-case basis.
Ex: If we take the wedge argument seriously, we would pass a law forbidding all vehicles to travel at any speed greater than 0. Easy way out of the moral problem. But we pass no such law.

Second-order thinking - Allows us to consider time, scale, thresholds and more. Weighing different paths is what thinking is all about.

### 6. Probabilistic thinking

The theory of probability is the only mathematical tool available to help map the unknown and the uncontrollable. It is fortunate that this tool, while tricky, is extraordinarily powerful and convenient. 

We know now that the future is inherently unpredictable because not all variables can be known and even the smallest error imaginable in our data very quickly throws off our predictions. The best we can do is estimate the future by generating realistic, useful probabilities.

*Three important aspects of Probability*:
1. Bayesian thinking - adjust probabilities when we get new data
2. Fat-tailed curves - no cap on extremes; Crazy things are definitely going to happen, we have no way of identifying when.
3. Asymmetries - Metaprobaility; Far more probability estimates are wrong on the “over-optimistic” side than the “under-optimistic” side.

*Additional Notes*

Think about: What the relevant priors? What might I already know that I can use to better understand the reality of the situation. 
Conditional probability: Be very careful to observe the conditions preceding an event you'd like to understand. 

Bell curve situation, we can quickly identify our parameters and plan for the most likely outcomes. Extremes are predictable. 

Question - Suppose you hear that you had a greater risk of slipping on the stairs and cracking your head open than being killed by a terrorist. The statistics, the priors, seem to back it up: 1,000 people slipped on the stairs and died last year in your country and only 500 died of terrorism. Should you be more worried about stairs or terror events?

Dealing with Fat-tailed distributions: Important: don't sit down and imagine every possible scenario in the tail. Position ourselves better to survive or even benefit from the widely unpredictable future - think correctly and plan for the world we don't fully understand.  

People overestimate their confidence in their probability estimates. Ex: Estimate the effect of traffic on travel time. Leave "on-time" & arrive 20% early - almost never; arrive 20% late - all the time. 

You’ll rarely read about an investor who aimed for 25% annual return rates who subsequently earned 40% over a long period of time. You can throw a dart at the Wall Street Journal and hit the names of lots of investors who aim for 25% per annum with each investment and end up closer to 10%.

> **Successful thinking in shades of probability** means roughly identifying what matters, coming up with a sense of the odds, doing a check on our assumptions, and then making a decision.

**Orders of Magnitude**:
Our naive use of probabilities. Black Swam by Nassim Taleb: Any small error in measuring the risk of an extreme event can mean we’re not just slightly off, but way off—off by orders of magnitude, in fact. In other words, not just 10% wrong but ten times wrong, or 100 times wrong, or 1,000 times wrong. Something we thought could only happen every 1,000 years might be likely to happen in any given year! This is using false prior information and results in us underestimating the probability of the future distribution being different.

**Anti-Fragility**:
How to benefit from the uncertainity? Fat-tails?
Three categories of objects: Ones that are harmed by volatility and unpredictability, ones that are neutral to volatility and unpredictability, and finally, ones that benefit from it.

Predict or Prepare? Prepare. Ways in which we can prepare:
1. Upside Optionality - Seeking out situations that we expect have a good odds of offering us opportunities (like attending a party with attendees of interest)
2. Learn how to fail properly - Never take a risk that will do you in completely. Develop the resilience to learn from failures and start again. 

The Antifragile mindset is a unique one. Whenever possible, try to create scenarios where randomness and uncertainty are your friends, not your enemies.

**Causation vs correlation**; regression to mean; Real effects of simple due to variability? Introducing control groups. Industry averages, peers in the cohort group or historical rates of improvement - are all imperfect measures.

### 7. Inversion

"The test of a first-rate intelligence is the ability to hold two opposing ideas in mind at the same time and still retain the ability to function. One should, for example, be able to see that things are hopeless yet be determined to make them otherwise."

Helps identify and remove obstacles to success. Avoiding stupidity is easier than seeking brilliance. Combining the ability to think forward and backward allows you to see reality from multiple angles.

Two approaches to applying inversion in your life:
1. Assume what I'm trying to prove is T/F. Show what else would have to be true. 
2. Think deeply about the things we want to avoid. Then see what options are left over. 

Sherlock Holmes find the picture of King with Irene Adler. 
Bernay: each time when “hired to sell a product or service, he instead sold whole new ways of behaving, which appeared obscure but over time reaped huge rewards for his clients and redefined the very texture of American life.

Making products on things we are trying to avoid. Like losses in stock market & poor money manager selection. 

**Idea of Force Field Analysis**:
Situation where change is desired, successful management of that change requires applied inversion. Here is a brief explanation of his process:
1. Identify the problem
2. Define your objective
3. Identify the forces that support change towards your objective
4. Identify the forces that impede change towards your objective
5. Strategize a solution!

Florence Nightingale: dropped death rate of British army in the Crimean war from 23% to 2.5% in 1 year. Statistically found the leading cause of death to be sanitation. Not only focused on what the army can do to improve outcomes, but also what they had to avoid to stop making things worse. Not how do we fix this problem but how do we stop it from happening in the first place. 

**Inversion leads to Innovation**
Identify end goal & work backwards. Mary Van Brittan Brown: safety conditions - adding more locks or inviting friends over. She identified that it was her inability to see and communicate with persons outside her door that made her feel the most vulnerable when home alone. Camera that would move through four holes in the door - feeding the images to the TV monitor setup. 

> Sun Tzu: Hence to fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy’s resistance without fighting.

### 8. Occam's Razor

> Anybody can make the simple complicated. Creativity is making the complicated simple. 

Simpler explanations are more likely to be true than complicated ones. Classic principle of logic & problem solving. 

We often spend lots of time coming up with very complicated narratives to explain what we see around us, it is significantly more likely that there are simpler and common explanations to it. 

Occam’s Razor is a great tool for avoiding unnecessary complexity by helping you identify and commit to the simplest explanation possible
Something that is easier to falsify, easier to understand and generally more likely to be correct. It's not iron law but tendency & mind-frame we can choose to use.

David Hume's opinion on Miracles (something that happens outside our understanding of how the nature works) - the most simple explanation for a miracle is that the
miracle-witnesser is not describing the event correctly, or the miracle represents a more common phenomenon that we currently don’t properly understand.

Vera Rubin in 1970s - had a bunch of data about the behavior of galaxies piling up that wasn’t explained by contemporary theories; Andromeda Galaxy violated Newton's laws of motion. Presence of Dark matter is the simplest explanation. 

Dark matter is an excellent theory with a lot of explanatory power. 
**Extraordinary claims require extraordinary proof**

Three million UV deflecting black balls.
Tigers killing villagers. A mask behind the face. 

Counter to Occam's Razor - Somethings are not that simple like building an aircraft. 
Razor in Leadership - last thing IBM needed was vision. What was needed? Serve customers, compete for business here and now, & focus on businesses that are already profitable. Needed simple, tough-minded business execution.

### 9. Hanlon's Razor

> I need to listen well 
so that I hear what is not said. 
We should not attribute to malice that which is more easily explained by stupidity.

Simplified to an extent that it still provides as actual understanding - like writing simple code. 
**Fallacy of Conjunction**

When we see something we don’t like happen and which seems wrong, we assume it’s intentional. But it’s more likely that it’s completely unintentional. Assuming someone is doing wrong and doing it purposefully is like assuming Linda is more likely to be a bank teller and a feminist. Most people doing wrong are not bad people trying to be malicious. **This helps us diagnose the causes of bad situation**

Hanlon’s Razor, when practiced diligently as a counter to confirmation bias, empowers us, and gives us far more realistic and effective options for remedying bad situations.

When we assume someone is out to get us, our very natural instinct is to take actions to defend ourselves. It’s harder to take advantage of, or even see, opportunities while in this defensive mode because our priority is saving ourselves—which tends to reduce our vision to dealing with the perceived threat instead of examining the bigger picture.

Ex: Vasili Arkhipov single-handedly avoided nuclear war with the Americans

**Devil Fallacy** - “I would say you’ve fallen into the commonest fallacy of all in dealing with social and economic subjects—the ‘devil’ theory. You have attributed conditions to villainy that simply result from stupidity…. You think bankers are scoundrels. They are not. Nor are company officials, nor patrons, nor the governing classes back on earth. Men are constrained by necessity and build up rationalizations to account for their acts.”

> All humans make mistakes and fall into traps of laziness, bad thinking, and bad incentives. Our lives are easier, better, and more effective when we recognize this truth and act accordingly.
