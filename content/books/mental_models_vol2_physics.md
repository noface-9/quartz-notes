### 1. Relativity

> Steven Strogatz: The theory of relativity is founded on empathy. Not empathy in the ordinary emotional sense; empathy in a rigorous scientific sense. The crucial idea is to imagine how things would appear to someone who’s moving in a different way than you are.

Here we are not talking about validity or invalidity - it's that we might not have the most complete view into a problem or situation.

##### Two thought experiments that explains science of relativity
1. Galileo - dropping a ball in the moving ship (perspective influences what we perceive as reality and how we understand the world)
2. Albert Einstein - Lightning strike at two corners of the train at once percieved by an outsider and an insider

There is an objective reality but none of us can perceive it without doing a little work. 
We make subopitmal decisions too because of this limitation in perception. 

Must recognise what these limits are and, in situations where the risks are high or the outcomes important, take steps to augment your perception. 

> Seeing with two eyes offers a synthesized view that’s more complete than either eye can see individually. This concept helps us understand that integrating multiple perspectives gives us a better view.

Our memories are highly subjective and malleable. People rewrite and reshape their memories, often to fit their existing beliefs. 

*What can we do to augment our perspective?* - Usage of thought experiments (need not be literal - don't have to be confined to the possibilites)
When you see someone doing something that doesn’t make sense to you, ask yourself what the world would have to look like to you for those actions to make sense.  
Traveling to new places far outside our normal experiences can jolt us into remembering our train, seeing it in a new light, understanding better its size and shape, and remind us that not everyone is on it.

> Descartes: It is good to know something of the customs of various peoples, so as to judge our own more soundly and so as not to think that everything that is contrary to our ways is ridiculous and against reason, as those who have seen nothing have a habit of doing.

*Bnefits* - get a more complete picture of the context in which you are operating and where opportunities might be. We all get stuck, frustrated when no one seems to understand us, or angry that we aren’t making progress based on how we understand the world. This is when it is most helpful to remember relativity and refresh our perspective by stepping outside of ourselves for a little bit.

### 2. Reciprocity

Teaches why win-win situations are the way to go. 

> Do unto others knowing that something will be done unto you.  
> For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. 

Newton's third law explains "propulsion" - forward drive. This works by forcing matter, such as gas produced by burning fuel, in one direction, leading to a corresponding movement of the vehicle in the opposite direction. 

Quid pro quo - Something for something, give and take, tit for tat

Norman Bethune - Canadian Surgeon - volunteer for a cause; Bethune volunteered his time, energy, and intelligence to try to bring about meaningful improvements in the lives of the most impoverished. Benefit is better conceptualized as the reciprocity received by the individual in terms of the satisfaction they have regarding the choices they’ve made. 

**Loss Aversion** - Governs the value of outcomes. The loss aversion coefficient is 1:2.5; We are driven more strongly to avoid losses than to achieve gains. *This is why putting ourselves out there, engaging people who might dismiss or reject us, is so scary.Because in the one-off situation, the pain it will cause is perceived as stronger than the positive feelings of acceptance. The trick is to start looking at outcomes in the aggregate instead of focusing on each unique situation.*

In the physical world - the law of reciprocity works 100% of the time. In the biological world, reciprocity doesn't have the same record. However, it has been discovered to work much more often than not, and thus harnessing it has significant long-term benefits. The fact that the human species has made it
to now is directly dependent on our building social interactions that are reliable, useful, and trustworthy. The small loss we occasionally experience as a result of putting ourselves out there and not having it reciprocated is more than compensated for by the gains the rest of the time.

The reality is, if you’re fighting with everyone all of the time you have to spread your resources along many fronts and you likely don’t have time to do anything else. 

**Schedenfreude** - damage joy; satisfaction at another person's misfortune. This is linked to our reciprocity. We feel it strongest when someone’s misfortune
seems earned, as penance for their misdeeds. Schadenfreude is tied to three things: aggression (us vs them), rivalry (feeling of superiority), and justice (serves them right).

Conclusion: Thus, to change our world, we must change what we offer to others. Reciprocity teaches us to be mindful of how our actions tend to come back on us. It’s important to remember that we are part of the world, and thus our actions do not happen in isolation, but are instead part of an interconnected web of effects.

### 3. Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics helps us understand randomness and disorder in systems and explains the conversion of energy from one form to another, the direction in which heat will flow, and the availability of energy to do work.

##### 4 laws of Thermodynamics:
1. Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transferred or changed from one form to another. There are two forms of energy exchange—heat and work. Heat is energy exchange through thermal interaction and work is energy exchange by any process other than heat. Whereas work can be completely converted into heat, heat cannot be completely converted to work.

2. Entropy of an isolated system always increases (Energy that is unabled to be used to do work). Isolated systems spontaneously progresses toward the state of maximum entropy - thermal equilibrium with nn net heat flow between objects. The entropy of the universe only increases with time. Impacts of this law is that we need to expend energy to create order. Without the deployment of energy, all things move away from order.

3. As temperature approaches absolute zero, the entropy of a given system approaches a constant value.

4. Fourth law is also known as zeroth law. If two objects are in thermal equilibrium with a third object, then those two objects are also in thermal equilibrium with each other.

Metaphorical applications:
1. Achieving success: Big fish in a small pond. Demonstrate our value in relation to those around us. 
2. Influence of behaviour of those around us. 
3. Entropy: energy is required to maintain order. Need to anticipate things falling apart and focus on prevention. 

Temperature is the crucial link between energy & time (two components of power). 
> Geerat Vermeij: The energy state of an economy—that is, its temperature—largely determines what its members can do and how fast they can do it. Temperature — the average kinetic energy of the moving molecules in a gas—affects every chemical process and every physical property associated with life. It influences not only the cost of doing business, but the speed at which tasks can be accomplished, and perhaps most importantly the range of adaptive options available. 

**Putting up walls**

Thermodynamics is about equilibrium (two systems of different temperatures, when exposed to the other, eventually becomes the same temperature). Usage of Insulators to slow down the temperature change but cannot stop it completely. 

> Helen Czerski: The physical world, all of it, only ever has one destination: equilibrium. 

When we talk about equilibrium of two systems - not just between two containers of different temperature of water, but two societies with different values. If we want to encourage equilibrium - then we think sharing as transfer of energy. 

Three physical modes to transfer energy: Radiation (transfers energy without touching anything), convection (material itself moves, carrying heat) and conduction (heat passing through direct contact). For example, radio and TV radiate ideas across borders. Teacher-student exchanges act as intellectual and social convection currents. Brands and foreign aid conduct values. Mixing cultures gives them common ground. 

Humans keep putting up border walls. Us vs them, my land-your land, our values-your values, our resources-your resources. But contrast is hard to maintain.  
A group of people will undergo changes in custom based on who they interact with outside their group. Walls are expensive to build and maintain.

Hadrian wall: to facilitate interaction with potentially hostile tribes. 
The Great wall of China: to claim territory and preserve culture. 
Berlin wall: in contrast, was to completely stop the movement of ideas as well as people.  
The concept of equilibrium is a useful lens to understanding the inevitable fall of physical walls that humans have built around the globe. It is difficult to prevent two cultures in direct contact from sharing ideas and customs.

**We don't like Disorder**

Why is reducing entropy so important? It's easier to have a mess of things since there are more ways for things to be messed up than sorted. To the extent that chance is operating, it is likely that a closed system that has some order will move toward disorder, which offers so many more possibilities.

The Value of Contrast: Only if there is contrast - there is development. If all the forces are balanced, a true state of equilibrium, there is no growth, no movement. 

> John Yorke: Art is born out of as well as encapsulates the continuing battle between order and chaos. It seeks order or form, even when portraying anarchy
At the heart of every story is the drumroll moment — the turning point when everything changes, and the characters must go on a journey to restore normality. As if we can slow down entropy by telling stories to reduce disorder.

The world is often messy, random, and difficult to understand. Humans naturally create stories to explain what happens. These stories give us a sense of order, help us make meaning from our experiences, and allow groups of people to cooperate and maintain stable societies.

### Understanding this intuitively

**What is entropy?**
In physics, entropy is a measure of disorder or randomness.
Imagine two bedrooms: 
Room A - Everything is neatly organized: Books on the shelf, Clothes folded, Bed made - This is low entropy.
Room B - Everything is scattered: Clothes everywhere, Books on the floor, Papers mixed together - This is high entropy.
Without anyone cleaning it, Room A naturally tends to become more like Room B over time.
Nature tends toward increasing entropy unless energy is continuously used to create order.

**Why is high entropy "incompatible with life"?**
Living organisms are incredibly organized.
Your body has: cells, organs, DNA, proteins, chemical reactions - Everything has to happen in a very precise way.
If everything became random: your cells wouldn't function, your heart wouldn't beat properly, your brain couldn't process information
Life constantly spends energy to maintain order.
A tree uses sunlight; You use food; We are all fighting entropy every day.

**Using entropy as a metaphor**
We are no longer talking about physics. We're talking about human minds.
Imagine this situation: I suddenly lose your job. Without explanation, my brain experiences:
"What happened?", "Why me?", "What's next?"
This feels like mental chaos. Our brain dislikes unexplained randomness.

**So what do humans do?**
We create stories.
For example: Instead of "Random bad things happen." we often think "Maybe this happened so I could find something better." or "This experience taught me resilience."
The story may or may not be objectively true. But it creates order.

Another example: Imagine someone gets rejected. Pure randomness feels painful. So they create a narrative: "This relationship wasn't right for me." or "I needed to grow first." The narrative organizes experience.

**Why are narratives so powerful?**
Humans don't just remember facts. We remember stories. Stories connect: Cause → Effect; Beginning → Middle → End; Without stories, life feels like disconnected events. Stories make life understandable.

**Society does the same thing**
Entire cultures are built on shared stories.
Examples: Justice matters; Hard work is rewarded; Families should support each other; Democracy is valuable; Education improves society.
These are narratives that help millions of strangers cooperate.

Imagine if everyone suddenly believed: "Nothing matters." -> People would stop trusting; Stop cooperating; Social order would weaken; Stories give people common expectations.

Life is often confusing. Random. Unfair. Unpredictable. Stories help us organize those experiences into something meaningful.

### 4. Inertia

Starting something is hard, but so is stopping something. In physics, inertia refers to the resistance a physical object has to a change in its state of motion. 

> Descartes: each thing, as far as is in its power, always remains in the same state; and that consequently, when it is once moved, it always continues to move.

This model is useful to understand our thinking patterns and habits. Our natural inclination to reject the new is in part normal resistance to the effort required to change. Keeping things as they are requires almost no effort and involves little uncertainty. We need force to effect change, and force requires effort. This model offers a lens to help us understand resistance to change and why we fail ourselves when we get complacent.

Lead and Absinthe - harmful effects, weights and discontinual consequences. 

*Inertia in War* - Easier to get well-rested men to move than exhausted men. *Escape Velocity* - Speed they need to reach to break away from gravitational force of a large body. 7 Miles per second on the surface of the earth. *Activation Energy* - effort needed not only to overcome resistance, but to set an object on a new path. 

Belief systems have enormous evolutionary inertia behind them. Our capacity for belief has been one of our survival mechanisms and that this biological relevance helps explain why beliefs are so resistant to change. Thus, sometimes the inertia of our beliefs hinders us, such as when they make us blind to new opportunities. Or when we dismiss new information or ideas because they don’t fit with what we think we know about the world.

Story about: Lise Meitner - Austrian physicist

Energy is precious and we employ it sparingly. It’s human nature to allow the current state to remain as changing it requires us to expend energy. Getting started is the hardest part. Once something is moving in a direction, it’s much easier to keep it in motion. But once something is in motion, it’s hard to stop. The bigger the mass the more effort required.

### 5. Friction and Viscosity

Friction is a force that must be overcome to achieve an outcome. There is always something trying to slow us down. All objects experience friciton. There are two key types of friction: Kinetic & Static. Kinetic Friction when two objects are sliding past each other. Static Friction occurs when an object is stationary; it prevents it from moving. 

Viscosity is the partner of Friction - It is the measure of how hard it is for one layer of fluid to slide over another layer.
If a liquid is hard to move it is more viscous - more resistant. 

For Plankton, viscosity matters. But for a whale due to its sheer size capitalises on other forces such as inertia 

Two important aspects: 
1. What is easy in one environment might be harder in another (ex: time of peace vs war).   
2. The main forces relevant to a particular situation depend on the scale you are operating at.   

**Slowing the flow** - 
Why does some information get disseminated quickly, whereas other times it gets bogged down and seems to go nowhere? Content of the information > environment
Example of Soviet Union convering my Chernobyl accident leading to so much chaos which could've otherwise been avoided. 

**Tricle up Innovation** - 
Usefulness of opposing forces like Friction & Viscosity: Organizational Effectiveness. 
Forces that influence innovation are different for the executive team and the frontline worker. Goal: encourage more innovation on the front lines, then you need to pay attention to what encourags and limits movement in that environment, not in the C-suite. 

Ford Model T left two legacies: the iconic image of the beginning of the automobile age, and the mass production system. Workers just did the repetitive tasks, leaving any re-work or problem solving to specialists. Whereas Toyota development guru **Taiichi Ohno** focused on the environment of the frontline worker.   
“If workers failed to anticipate problems before they occurred, and didn’t take the initiative to devise solutions, the work of the whole factory could easily come to a halt.": Responsibility of minor repairs and QC was given. Can stop the line if there emerged a problem that couldn't be fixed. Special time allocated to work on improving the processes and share ideas. 

Lean System encouraged them to be knowledgeable about the entire process and get curious about finding solutions and efficiencies. 

If you want to change a situation, you have to appreciate the forces that are strongest in that particular environment. Don't have to use more force at all times, we can also work on reducing the friciton.

### 6. Velocity

Velocity is often confused with speed. Speed is just movement - even running at a place; Velocity has direction - must go somewhere. Important to pay attention to where we are going and not how fast you are moving. Same speed & same direction --> constant velocity (in the right direction is the most effective strategy to get where you want to go). Too many changes in the direction - you may end up going in circles. 

> Seneca: If a man does not know to what port he is steering, no wind is favorable to him. 

Kinetic energy is energy because something is moving. Potential energy is energy because something has the ability to move due to its position or shape. As objects move, energy constantly transforms between these two forms, but the total energy remains the same ignoring the air resistance. Kinetic energy is relative to the observer, where potential energy depends on the where the object is or it's shape/form. 

**Faster to the Goal** - 
> Napolean: The strength of the army like power in mechanics, is the product of multiplying the mass by the velocity

Part of what allowed Napolean to move fast while on this campaign “was a profound study of the history and geography of Italy before he ever set foot there and his willingness to experiment with other’s ideas.” He developed expertise of the territory which gave him flexibility, and he chose his path so as to maintain as constant a velocity as possible. 

1. His treatment not only made them fighting men, but also his men. Giving them victory & talking to the men as equals. They admired Napolean and completely bought into his vision. 
2. He issued clear and simple instructions. In order for any strategy to work - he needed his ideas to be understood and executed. 

On his quest to Russian - his planning was inadequate. He was unable to adjust and adapt to the conditions he encountered. 
When we need to achieve any goal, the speed at which we move in that direction is not the only factor, because time is not the only component of success. 

Figuring out how to improve your velocity must take into account the full scope of what you want arrival at your destination to look like. Better go the right direction slowly than the wrong direction with speed.

**Eye on the Prize** - 
Mae West's transformation from struggling performer to creator, producer, and star of her own scripts came slowly, and required a major mobilization; getting there took concentrated energy, belief in her own abilities, sweat and well-worked connections. She approached her career focused on the long game. She knew the direction she was headed and made decisions to increase her velocity in that direction.

### 7. Leverage

Achieving results significantly greater than the force we put in. Making something light by raising it in a specific manner - using a lever. 

Examples: written language, standardization of processes. 

**Understanding where we have leverage**

Leverage is not manipulation, it is influence. In negotiations, having the leverage increases the chances that you will get a desired outcome. 
Two of the principles of leverage in negotiations. The first is that leverage in human interactions is based on perceptions and second that it is a social or relational construct. 

**Applying leverage where it counts**

When it comes to leverage, we want to know three things:
1. How do I know when I have it?
2. Where and when should I apply it?
3. How do I keep it?

Story of Eleanor of Aquitaine - Queen of France and then England in 12th Century - no access to solitary force and had to completely rely on leverage to attain and maintain power. 

Bad example: Exploitation of Coal company in West Virginia. Wielding leverage to maximum effect all of the time, as the West Virginia mine owners did, sows the seeds of ongoing unrest that undermine one’s ability to be truly effective.

Leverage is best paired with reciprocity—building in win-win thinking helps you keep your leverage sustainable and achieve better, longer lasting, outcomes.
